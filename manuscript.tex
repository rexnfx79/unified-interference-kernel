\begin{abstract}
We present a framework for organizing Yukawa couplings through envelope suppression and phase interference. A single kernel \emph{form} is reused across fermion sectors as a phenomenological parameterization, not as a derived QFT mechanism. The primary finding is that the quark sector exhibits a structural CKM--$m_c$ trade-off (nondominated Pareto frontier) while failing holdout generalization: pre-registered train/holdout diagnostics find \textbf{0\% strict survivors} across Gaussian, clockwork, and generalized envelopes (100 geometries). Charged-lepton and neutrino sectors show sector-specific partial success under \emph{different} optimization protocols---legacy full-objective sweeps report $\sim$60\% and $\sim$45\% survivor rates, whereas train/holdout splits yield 1\% strict lepton survivors (holdout $m_e$ structural failure), \textbf{27.8\% joint strict} neutrino survivors (PMNS + $\Delta m^2$, diagnostic 28), and \textbf{78.9\% PMNS-only strict} survivors (diagnostic 23, 100 geometries each)---the latter does not survive joint mass constraints. Exploratory parameter-scale clustering ($\sigma$, $\alpha$) is noted but likely reflects optimization bias. Companion-repository diagnostics refute universal kernel \emph{parameters} across sectors and operational quantum-information explanations of regime splits.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} flavor physics, Yukawa couplings, CKM matrix, PMNS matrix, split fermions, neutrino anarchy

\section{Introduction}

The flavor puzzle in the Standard Model---the origin of fermion masses and mixing angles---remains one of the most fundamental open questions in particle physics. Despite decades of theoretical effort, no compelling explanation has emerged for why the quark and lepton mixing matrices exhibit their observed hierarchical structures.

The split-fermion framework \citep{ArkaniHamed2000} provided an elegant geometric approach, positing that fermions are localized at different positions in an extra dimension, with Yukawa couplings determined by wavefunction overlaps. However, this approach alone cannot account for the full complexity of observed mixing patterns, particularly in the neutrino sector where mixing angles are large.

We propose that a unified interference kernel, combining envelope suppression with phase interference, can \emph{organize} Yukawa couplings across fermion sectors as a phenomenological parameterization. We use \textit{projection regime} labels (envelope-dominated for quarks, phase-sensitive for charged leptons, metric-dominated for neutrinos) to classify optimization setups and fitted parameter patterns---not as a validated sampling mechanism. Pre-registered diagnostics find \textbf{0\% strict quark survivors}, charged-lepton holdout $m_e$ failure under train/holdout splits, \textbf{27.8\% joint strict} neutrino success (PMNS + $\Delta m^2$) versus \textbf{78.9\% PMNS-only strict} when mass splittings are excluded from the objective.

This work presents optimization results across all three fermion sectors, documenting what the kernel can and cannot fit with pre-registered train/holdout splits. The quark sector fails holdout generalization (0\% strict survivors); charged leptons and neutrinos show sector-specific partial success whose survivor rates depend on objective protocol (full-objective legacy vs.\ train/holdout strict). We characterize Pareto boundary shapes and note exploratory envelope-compression patterns in neutrinos without claiming a validated three-regime sampling mechanism.

The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 introduces the unified interference kernel and three projection regimes. Section 3 describes the optimization methodology. Sections 4--5 present results for quarks and leptons, respectively. Section 6 discusses interpretations and limitations. Section 7 concludes.

This work treats the interference kernel as a \textbf{phenomenological parameterization} of Standard Model Yukawa textures. Pre-registered companion-repository diagnostics refute an operational quantum-information-theoretic explanation of sector-specific parameter splits (Fisher alignment $\approx 0.46$, frozen quark-to-lepton transfer loss $\gtrsim 800$, pooled open-system correlations $|r| \lesssim 0.01$). \textbf{Universal kernel parameters across fermion sectors are ruled out}; only the functional form is reused. For quarks, optimization on training targets ($m_c$, $|V_{us}|$, $|V_{cb}|$) does not generalize to holdout observables ($m_u$, $m_d$, $m_s$, $|V_{ub}|$). Charged-lepton and neutrino archived survivor rates must not be read as validation of a three-regime sampling hypothesis while the quark sector fails.

\section{Model}

\subsection{Unified Interference Kernel}

The unified interference kernel organizes Yukawa couplings through a combination of envelope suppression and phase interference:

\begin{equation}
Y_{ij} = \exp\left(-\frac{d_{ij}^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \times \left[1 + \varepsilon \exp(i\Phi_{ij})\right],
\end{equation}

where $d_{ij} = |x_i - x_j|$ is the distance in an internal flavor coordinate (not spacetime), and $\Phi_{ij}$ is a phase structure:

\begin{equation}
\Phi_{ij} = \alpha + k\frac{x_i + x_j}{2} + \eta(x_i - x_j).
\end{equation}

The parameters are:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\sigma$: envelope width, controlling the range of overlap
\item $\varepsilon$: interference strength, controlling the amplitude of phase-dependent modulation
\item $\alpha, k, \eta$: phase parameters determining interference patterns
\item $x_i, x_j$: discrete coordinates in flavor space
\end{itemize}

The envelope term $\exp(-d_{ij}^2/(2\sigma^2))$ provides hierarchical suppression with distance, while the interference term $1 + \varepsilon \exp(i\Phi_{ij})$ introduces phase-dependent modulations that can enhance or suppress couplings.

Throughout this work, ``universal'' refers to the reuse of a single kernel functional form across sectors, not to universality of parameter values, functional dependence, or UV origin. Similarly, terms such as ``sampling'' and ``information loss'' refer to effective properties of the Yukawa map under projection and optimization, not to dynamical processes in spacetime. These are structural descriptions within the model framework.

\subsection{Discrete Geometry}

Fermions are assigned discrete coordinates in a three-dimensional flavor space. This discrete geometry framework provides a minimal parameterization of flavor structure, with each coordinate assignment representing a distinct geometry.

For quarks, we have:
\begin{itemize}
\item Left-handed doublets: $(Q_1, Q_2, 0)$ where $Q_1, Q_2$ are integers
\item Up-type right-handed singlets: $(U_1, U_2, U_3)$ where $U_1, U_2, U_3$ are integers
\item Down-type right-handed singlets: $(D_1, D_2, D_3)$ where $D_1, D_2, D_3$ are integers
\end{itemize}

For charged leptons:
\begin{itemize}
\item Left-handed doublets: $(L_1, L_2, 0)$ where $L_1, L_2$ are integers
\item Right-handed singlets: $(E_1, E_2, E_3)$ where $E_1, E_2, E_3$ are integers
\end{itemize}

For neutrinos:
\begin{itemize}
\item Left-handed doublets: $(L_1, L_2, 0)$ (same as charged leptons)
\item Right-handed neutrinos: $(N_1, N_2, N_3)$ where $N_1, N_2, N_3$ are integers
\item Right-handed charged leptons: $(E_1, E_2, E_3)$ (affects PMNS via SVD)
\end{itemize}

The Yukawa matrix is constructed by evaluating the kernel between all pairs of left-handed and right-handed fermion coordinates. For example, for quarks, the up-type Yukawa matrix has elements:
\begin{equation}
Y_{u,ij} = K(Q_i, U_j; \sigma, k, \alpha, \eta, \varepsilon_u),
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the kernel function, and similarly for down-type quarks with coordinates $D_j$ and parameter $\varepsilon_d$.

The resulting $3 \times 3$ complex matrix is then diagonalized via singular value decomposition (SVD) to extract physical observables. For quark and lepton sectors, SVD yields:
\begin{equation}
Y = U \Sigma V^\dagger,
\end{equation}
where $U$ and $V$ are unitary matrices encoding mixing angles, and $\Sigma$ is a diagonal matrix encoding masses. For neutrinos, both the neutrino Yukawa matrix $Y_\nu$ and charged lepton Yukawa matrix $Y_e$ are diagonalized, and the PMNS mixing matrix is extracted as $U_{\text{PMNS}} = U_e^\dagger U_\nu$ where $U_e$ and $U_\nu$ come from SVD of $Y_e$ and $Y_\nu$ respectively.

\subsection{Three Projection Regimes}

We use three \emph{organizational labels}---envelope-dominated (quarks), phase-sensitive (charged leptons), and metric-dominated (neutrinos)---to classify sector-specific optimization setups and fitted parameter patterns. These labels are \textbf{not} validated as distinct sampling mechanisms of a universal kernel; pre-registered quark holdout diagnostics refute cross-sector mechanistic claims.

\textbf{Envelope-Dominated Label (Quarks):} Baseline kernel parameters $(\sigma, k, \alpha, \eta)$ with sector-specific interference strengths $(\varepsilon_u, \varepsilon_d)$. Envelope suppression is the primary hierarchy driver in this setup; phase interference provides subdominant corrections.

\textbf{Phase-Sensitive Label (Charged Leptons):} Variable phase parameters $(k_e, \eta_e)$ distinct from the quark baseline, with similar envelope parameters. This setup targets charged lepton masses under legacy full-objective or train/holdout protocols.

\textbf{Metric-Dominated Label (Neutrinos):} Envelope compression via $g_{\text{env}}$ with $\sigma_{\text{eff}} = \sigma \times g_{\text{env}}$. Charged lepton Yukawa matrices use lepton-specific phase parameters $(k_e, \eta_e, \varepsilon_e)$; neutrino Yukawa matrices use $(k, \eta, \varepsilon_\nu)$. Large PMNS mixing is \emph{described} in this setup but not established as caused by compression.

\subsection{Observables}

Physical observables are extracted from Yukawa matrices via singular value decomposition (SVD). The extraction procedure depends on the fermion sector:

\textbf{Quark Sector:} From the up- and down-type Yukawa matrices $Y_u$ and $Y_d$, we perform SVD:
\begin{equation}
Y_u = U_u \Sigma_u V_u^\dagger, \quad Y_d = U_d \Sigma_d V_d^\dagger,
\end{equation}
where $\Sigma_u$ and $\Sigma_d$ are diagonal matrices containing masses, and $U_u, V_u, U_d, V_d$ are unitary matrices. The CKM mixing matrix is:
\begin{equation}
V_{\text{CKM}} = U_u^\dagger U_d,
\end{equation}
from which we extract the mixing angles and CP-violating phase. We focus on the elements $V_{us}, V_{cb}, V_{ub}$ which are precisely measured.

Quark masses are extracted from $\Sigma_u$ and $\Sigma_d$. We compare to PDG 2024 values \citep{PDG2024}:
\begin{itemize}
\item Up-type: $m_u, m_c$ (charm quark mass $m_c = 1.27$ GeV is most important)
\item Down-type: $m_d, m_s, m_b$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Charged Lepton Sector:} From the charged lepton Yukawa matrix $Y_e$, we perform SVD:
\begin{equation}
Y_e = U_e \Sigma_e V_e^\dagger,
\end{equation}
where $\Sigma_e$ contains the charged lepton masses. We extract $m_e, m_\mu, m_\tau$ and compare to PDG 2024 values: $m_e = 0.000511$ GeV, $m_\mu = 0.105658$ GeV, $m_\tau = 1.77686$ GeV.

\textbf{Neutrino Sector:} Both neutrino and charged lepton Yukawa matrices are diagonalized:
\begin{equation}
Y_\nu = U_\nu \Sigma_\nu V_\nu^\dagger, \quad Y_e = U_e \Sigma_e V_e^\dagger.
\end{equation}
The PMNS mixing matrix is:
\begin{equation}
U_{\text{PMNS}} = U_e^\dagger U_\nu,
\end{equation}
from which we extract mixing angles $\theta_{12}, \theta_{23}, \theta_{13}$ using the standard parametrization. We also extract neutrino masses from $\Sigma_\nu$ and compute mass-squared differences $\Delta m_{21}^2, \Delta m_{31}^2$.

\textbf{Loss Functions:} We define loss functions to compare predictions to experimental values. For mixing angles, we use relative squared errors:
\begin{equation}
L_{\text{mixing}} = \sum_{i} \left(\frac{O_i^{\text{pred}} - O_i^{\text{exp}}}{O_i^{\text{exp}}}\right)^2,
\end{equation}
where $O_i$ are mixing matrix elements or angles. For masses, we use squared log-ratio errors:
\begin{equation}
L_{\text{mass}} = \sum_{i} \left(\log\frac{m_i^{\text{pred}}}{m_i^{\text{exp}}}\right)^2,
\end{equation}
which is sensitive to relative errors and appropriate for hierarchical masses spanning many orders of magnitude.

\subsection{Scope and Epistemological Framework}

This model operates at the effective field theory level, providing a structural framework for organizing Yukawa couplings rather than a fundamental theory. The claims are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A single universal kernel form can organize all Yukawa couplings
\item Different fermion sectors correspond to different projection regimes
\item Large neutrino mixing correlates with metric-dominated projection (envelope compression)
\end{enumerate}

These are structural observations within this model class, not claims about fundamental physics. The model provides a framework for understanding flavor patterns, but makes no claims about UV completion or underlying microscopic theory.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Optimization Strategy}

We employ differential evolution \citep{Storn1997} to optimize kernel parameters for each geometry (discrete coordinate assignment). For each sector, we:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Generate discrete geometries systematically
\item For each geometry, optimize continuous parameters $(\sigma, k, \alpha, \eta, \varepsilon)$
\item Use multiple random seeds (typically 5) to ensure robustness
\item Select best result across seeds for each geometry
\end{enumerate}

The optimization minimizes a combined loss function:
\begin{equation}
L_{\text{total}} = L_{\text{mass}} + \lambda L_{\text{mixing}},
\end{equation}
where $L_{\text{mass}}$ is the sum of squared log-ratio errors for masses, $L_{\text{mixing}}$ is the sum of relative squared errors for mixing angles, and $\lambda$ is a weighting factor (typically 5.0) to balance the two objectives.

We use the following hyperparameters:
\begin{itemize}
\item Maximum iterations: 200 per optimization
\item Random seeds: 5 per geometry
\item Population size: 15 $\times$ number of parameters (differential evolution default)
\item Tolerance: $10^{-6}$ for convergence
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sector-Specific Methods}

\subsubsection{Quark Sector Optimization}

We optimize over discrete geometries with $Q, U, D$ coordinates up to maximum coordinate value 5, generating 1000 geometries. The discrete coordinates are systematically enumerated, with each geometry representing a unique assignment of flavor space positions to the three generations of quarks.

For each geometry, we optimize six continuous parameters: $(\sigma, k, \alpha, \eta, \varepsilon_u, \varepsilon_d)$. The parameter bounds are chosen based on prior exploratory analysis:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\sigma \in [0.5, 6.0]$: Envelope width, controlling the range of overlap
\item $k \in [0.1, 2.0]$: Phase parameter controlling symmetric part of phase
\item $\alpha \in [0, 2\pi]$: Global phase shift
\item $\eta \in [1.0, 5.0]$: Phase parameter controlling antisymmetric part
\item $\varepsilon_u, \varepsilon_d \in [0.01, 0.5]$: Interference strengths for up- and down-type quarks
\end{itemize}

The objective function combines CKM mixing loss and mass loss:
\begin{equation}
L_{\text{quark}} = L_{\text{mass}} + 5.0 \times L_{\text{CKM}} + L_{\text{penalty}},
\end{equation}
where $L_{\text{mass}}$ is the sum of squared log-ratio errors for quark masses, $L_{\text{CKM}}$ is the sum of relative squared errors for CKM matrix elements $V_{us}, V_{cb}, V_{ub}$, and $L_{\text{penalty}}$ includes penalties for masses falling outside acceptable ranges.

Each geometry is optimized with 5 different random seeds to ensure robustness against local minima, and the best result across seeds is retained.

\subsubsection{Charged Lepton Sector Optimization}

We optimize over 100 geometries with $L, E$ coordinates, where $L = (L_1, L_2, 0)$ represents left-handed lepton doublets and $E = (E_1, E_2, E_3)$ represents right-handed lepton singlets.

The phase-sensitive regime uses five parameters: $(\sigma, k_e, \alpha, \eta_e, \varepsilon_e)$ where $k_e$ and $\eta_e$ are phase parameters specific to leptons, allowing variation from the quark baseline. This phase variation is crucial for resolving the muon mass hierarchy, as the phase-sensitive regime enables fine-tuning of interference patterns.

The objective function focuses on charged lepton masses:
\begin{equation}
L_{\text{lepton}} = L_{\text{mass}} + L_{\text{penalty}},
\end{equation}
where $L_{\text{mass}}$ includes errors for $m_e, m_\mu, m_\tau$, and penalties ensure masses fall within acceptable ranges. The phase-sensitive regime achieves exceptional precision, with 60\% of geometries matching all experimental mass ranges simultaneously.

Each optimization uses 5 random seeds with 200 iterations per seed, with the best result retained.

\subsubsection{Neutrino Sector Optimization}

We optimize over 96 geometries $\times$ 5 values of $g_{\text{env}} \in [0.5, 0.6, 0.65, 0.7]$ (5 discrete steps), totaling 480 optimizations. The envelope compression factor $g_{\text{env}}$ controls the effective envelope width $\sigma_{\text{eff}} = \sigma \times g_{\text{env}}$.

The metric-dominated regime requires nine parameters: $(\sigma, k, \alpha, \eta, \varepsilon_\nu, k_e, \eta_e, \varepsilon_e, g_{\text{env}})$ where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $k, \eta, \varepsilon_\nu$ control the neutrino Yukawa matrix $Y_\nu$
\item $k_e, \eta_e, \varepsilon_e$ control the charged lepton Yukawa matrix $Y_e$ (which affects PMNS extraction via SVD)
\item $g_{\text{env}}$ controls envelope compression for the neutrino sector
\end{itemize}

The objective function combines PMNS mixing loss and neutrino mass loss:
\begin{equation}
L_{\text{neutrino}} = L_{\text{mass}} + 5.0 \times L_{\text{PMNS}} + L_{\text{m1,penalty}},
\end{equation}
where $L_{\text{PMNS}}$ includes errors for $\theta_{12}, \theta_{23}, \theta_{13}$, and $L_{\text{m1,penalty}}$ ensures the lightest neutrino mass falls within acceptable bounds.

The compression factor $g_{\text{env}} < 1$ correlates weakly with large mixing angles in exploratory fits. Under \emph{legacy full-objective} optimization (480 geometries, all PMNS angles in the loss), optimal compression occurs at $g_{\text{env}} \approx 0.5$--0.6 and 45\% of geometries match experimental PMNS angles at 10\% tolerance; pre-registered strict PMNS-only diagnostics (diagnostic 23, 100 geometries) report 78.9\% survivors.

\section{Results: Quark Sector}

\subsection{Pre-Registered Quark Diagnostics}

Quark-sector results are reported from pre-registered diagnostics only (not cherry-picked best geometries from legacy 1000-geometry archives).

\textbf{Diagnostic 21 (train/holdout, $N=100$):} Optimize $m_c$, $|V_{us}|$, $|V_{cb}|$; hold out $m_u$, $m_d$, $m_s$, $|V_{ub}|$. Across Gaussian, clockwork, and generalized envelopes: \textbf{0\% strict survivors} (0/100 per kernel). Median holdout loss $\approx 33$ (Gaussian); CKM--$m_c$ Pareto structure persists (correlation $\approx 0.01$).

\textbf{Diagnostic 27 (Tier A2 joint loss, $N=100$):} All seven quark observables in the optimization objective. Gaussian kernel: \textbf{2.0\% strict survivors} (2/100); clockwork and generalized: 0\%. CKM--$m_c$ Pareto correlation $\approx 0.10$ persists---joint optimization is not the sole cause of failure. Median holdout-equivalent loss drops to $\approx 0.69$ vs.\ $\approx 33$ under the split objective, but simultaneous PDG-relative match remains negligible.

\textbf{Diagnostics 29--30 (geometry extension, unified joint protocol):} Extended grids (100 sampled extensions; 5759 legacy-re-baseline + shell-5 exhaustive) yield \textbf{0/5759 strict survivors}. Marginal joint-loss improvements do not produce simultaneous PDG match (Wilson 95\% upper bound on strict rate $\lesssim 0.001$).

Legacy 1000-geometry archives show individual CKM elements can be fit precisely while $m_c$ remains order-of-magnitude wrong ($m_c = 19.4 \pm 17.9$ GeV vs.\ 1.27 GeV target); pre-registered diagnostics confirm this is structural, not an artifact of geometry sampling or train/holdout splitting alone.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}

\textbf{The quark sector model fails.} Analysis of 1000 random geometries reveals that the envelope-dominated regime cannot reproduce experimental quark observables (Fig.~\ref{fig:quark_failure}). The charm quark mass prediction is $m_c = 19.4 \pm 17.9$ GeV, compared to the experimental target of $m_c = 1.27$ GeV---a systematic error of 1425\%. No geometry achieves $m_c$ within even 100\% of the target. CKM matrix elements show similarly large systematic errors. This failure persists across all parameter combinations, suggesting that the envelope-dominated ansatz is fundamentally inappropriate for the quark sector.

\subsection{Parameter Attribution}

Optimal parameters for quark sector geometries cluster around:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\sigma = 4.99 \pm 1.18$ (range: 0.59--6.00)
\item $k = 1.40 \pm 0.57$ (range: 0.10--2.00)
\item $\alpha = 2.98 \pm 1.58$ (range: 0.00--6.28)
\item $\eta = 2.95 \pm 1.47$ (range: 1.00--5.00)
\item $\varepsilon_u = 0.14 \pm 0.16$ (range: 0.01--0.50)
\item $\varepsilon_d = 0.20 \pm 0.19$ (range: 0.01--0.50)
\end{itemize}

The envelope width $\sigma \approx 5$ and phase parameter $\alpha \approx \pi$ emerge as natural scales for the quark sector.

\section{Results: Lepton Sectors}

\subsection{Charged Leptons: Phase-Sensitive Regime}

The phase-sensitive regime for charged leptons achieves strong performance under \emph{legacy} full-objective optimization (all three masses in the loss): a 60\% survivor rate (60 out of 100 geometries matching all experimental mass ranges at 10\% tolerance). All legacy survivors achieve perfect mass matches with errors $< 0.01\%$. Pre-registered train/holdout diagnostics (optimize $m_\mu + m_\tau$, hold out $m_e$; diagnostic 22, 100 geometries) find only 1\% strict survivors---holdout $m_e$ fails structurally even when training masses are perfect.

The best geometry achieves a total loss of $2.45 \times 10^{-11}$, with mass predictions:
\begin{itemize}
\item $m_e = 0.000511$ GeV (target: 0.000511 GeV) --- perfect match
\item $m_\mu = 0.105657$ GeV (target: 0.105658 GeV) --- 0.00\% error
\item $m_\tau = 1.776860$ GeV (target: 1.776860 GeV) --- perfect match
\end{itemize}

Z-scores for legacy survivors are excellent: $Z(m_e) = 0.10$, $Z(m_\mu) = 0.27$, $Z(m_\tau) = 0.85$, all well below 1.0, indicating agreement within $1\sigma$.

\subsection{Neutrinos: Metric-Dominated Regime}

The metric-dominated regime for neutrinos achieves a 45\% survivor rate under \emph{legacy} full-objective optimization (216 out of 480 optimizations matching all PMNS angles at 10\% tolerance). Pre-registered train/holdout strict diagnostics (diagnostic 23, 100 geometries) find 78.9\% PMNS survivors. PMNS angle matches among legacy survivors are excellent:

\item $\theta_{13} = 0.1490 \pm 0.0008$ (target: 0.149) --- 0.02\% error
\end{itemize}

The best geometry achieves a PMNS loss of $1.36 \times 10^{-8}$, with angle predictions matching experimental values to within 0.02--2.40\%. Z-scores for legacy survivors are excellent: $Z(\theta_{12}) = 0.62$, $Z(\theta_{23}) = 0.26$, $Z(\theta_{13}) = 0.05$, all well below 1.0.

\subsection{Parameter Commonalities Across Sectors}

Analysis across all three sectors reveals consistent parameter scales within optimization uncertainty:

\textbf{Envelope Width $\sigma$:} All sectors converge to $\sigma \approx 4$--5:
\begin{itemize}
\item Quark: $4.99 \pm 1.18$
\item Charged Lepton: $4.40 \pm 1.39$
\item Neutrino: $4.94 \pm 1.39$
\end{itemize}

This consistency in envelope width is suggestive but may reflect optimization bias toward interior parameter values given the bounded parameter ranges, rather than physical universality.

\textbf{Phase Parameter $\alpha$:} All sectors cluster around $\alpha \approx 2.5$--3.0 (near $\pi$):
\begin{itemize}
\item Quark: $2.98 \pm 1.58$
\item Charged Lepton: $2.58 \pm 1.55$
\item Neutrino: $2.46 \pm 1.04$
\end{itemize}

The clustering near $\pi$ suggests optimal phase interference occurs at this value, possibly corresponding to destructive interference patterns that create natural hierarchies.

\textbf{Envelope Suppression $\varepsilon$:} Shows systematic progression across regimes:
\begin{itemize}
\item Quark: $\varepsilon_u = 0.14$, $\varepsilon_d = 0.20$ (lowest)
\item Charged Lepton: $\varepsilon_e = 0.24$ (intermediate)
\item Neutrino: $\varepsilon_\nu = 0.44$, $\varepsilon_e = 0.36$ (highest)
\end{itemize}

This progression is \emph{descriptive} of fitted $\varepsilon$ values in each sector's optimization, not a validated prediction of the three-regime framework (which the quark holdout failure refutes as a cross-sector hypothesis).

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Honest Assessment}

\textbf{The three-regime framework is not validated by this data.} While the conceptual framework (Fig.~\ref{fig:three_regime}) proposes that different fermion sectors sample a universal kernel differently, the empirical results do not support this claim:

\textbf{Quark Sector: Complete Failure.} The envelope-dominated regime produces charm masses 15$\times$ larger than experiment. No parameter combination achieves even 100\% accuracy. This is not a ``trade-off'' to be characterized---it is a fundamental model failure (Fig.~\ref{fig:quark_failure}).

\textbf{Charged Lepton Sector: Partial Success.} Legacy full-objective sweeps report $\sim$60\% success at 10\% tolerance (Fig.~\ref{fig:success_threshold}). Pre-registered train/holdout diagnostics (optimize $m_\mu + m_\tau$, hold out $m_e$) find 1\% strict survivors at 100 geometries---holdout $m_e$ fails even when training masses are perfect. The muon mass shows large scatter, and phase-parameter correlations are not statistically significant.

\textbf{Neutrino Sector: Qualified Success.} Legacy full-objective sweeps report $\sim$39--45\% success at 10\% tolerance for mixing angles. Train/holdout strict diagnostics at 100 geometries find 78.9\% PMNS survivors; however, 50\% of legacy optimizations failed entirely, and $g_{\text{env}}$--mixing correlations remain weak ($|r| < 0.1$ on best-per-geometry records).

The consistent parameter scales ($\sigma \approx 4$--5, $\alpha \approx \pi$) likely reflect optimization bias toward interior parameter values rather than physical universality.

\subsection{Pre-Registered Falsifiers}

\textit{Reporting convention:} ``Pre-registered'' here denotes the repo-locked strict survivor protocol (companion repository wiki: \texttt{survivor-protocol-preregistered}), fixed geometry seeds, and Tier A/B falsifier scripts committed before result ingestion---not external clinical-trial-style preregistration on a third-party registry.

Tier~A2 (diagnostic~27) optimizes all seven quark observables jointly rather than the train/holdout split of diagnostic~21. Under this objective the Gaussian kernel achieves \textbf{2.0\% strict survivors} (2/100 geometries)---not zero, but still negligible---while the CKM--$m_c$ Pareto structure persists (correlation $\approx 0.1$). The split objective is therefore not the sole cause of quark failure, though holdout masses remain poorly predicted (median holdout loss $\approx 0.69$ vs.\ $\approx 33$ under the split objective).

Tier~B2 (diagnostic~28) adds $\Delta m^2$ to the neutrino optimization objective. PMNS-only strict success drops from \textbf{78.9\%} (diagnostic~23) to \textbf{27.8\%} (22/79 solved geometries); joint strict (angles + $\Delta m^2$) equals PMNS-only strict at 27.8\%. Neutrino phenomenology success is therefore largely \emph{PMNS-angle} success; mass splittings are not simultaneously fit at pre-registered tolerances.

Both results follow the strict survivor protocol in companion diagnostics and reinforce the honest assessment above: quark failure is structural, and neutrino success requires explicit PMNS-only caveats.

\subsection{Large Mixing and Envelope Compression}

Neutrino optimizations often use envelope compression ($g_{\text{env}} < 1$ on the effective width $\sigma_{\text{eff}} = \sigma \times g_{\text{env}}$). Fitted $g_{\text{env}}$ clusters near $\sim$0.45--0.50 in pre-registered sweeps (diagnostic 23), not the $\sim$0.60 reported in legacy archives. Correlations between $g_{\text{env}}$ and PMNS angles are weak ($|r| < 0.1$ with bootstrap confidence intervals overlapping zero).

We do \emph{not} claim that envelope compression \emph{causes} large neutrino mixing or that quark small mixing follows from the absence of compression. Proper anarchy tests---independence from geometry, comparison to Haar measure, entropy quantification---remain open. Quark holdout failure breaks any claim that a single parameterized framework unifies quark and neutrino mixing phenomenology.

\subsection{Comparison with Split-Fermion Models}

Extra-dimensional split-fermion constructions with localized zero modes and point interactions have demonstrated that fermion mass hierarchies, mixing, and CP violation can arise from geometric overlap effects rather than imposed flavor symmetries, including realizations for quarks \citep{Fujimoto2013} and leptons \citep{Fujimoto2014} based on generalized boundary conditions and twisted Higgs phases. Recent work has also addressed the dynamical origin of bulk mass parameters \citep{Angelescu2019} and Gaussian wavefunction overlaps in string compactifications \citep{Abe2016}. These models operate within a higher-dimensional gauge theory framework and rely on explicit boundary-condition engineering and sector-specific bulk parameters to reproduce observed flavor structure.

By contrast, the present work does not assume a literal extra spatial dimension or boundary-condition dynamics. Instead, we study an effective interference Yukawa kernel defined over an internal coordinate and document sector-specific fit outcomes under different optimization protocols. Our analysis emphasizes a structural CKM--$m_c$ Pareto envelope that persists across pre-registered kernel variants and geometry extensions (diagnostics 21, 27, 29--30), though it does \emph{not} yield strict simultaneous PDG match and should not be read as geometry-independent universality. In this sense, the present framework is complementary to split-fermion point-interaction models: while both exploit geometric localization, we focus on interference-driven organizational labels and documented constraint surfaces rather than on UV-specific boundary constructions.

\subsection{Scope and Limitations}

This model operates at the effective field theory level, providing a structural framework rather than a fundamental theory. The model makes no claims about:
\begin{itemize}
\item UV completion or underlying microscopic theory
\item Why the universal kernel takes this particular form
\item How the discrete geometry arises from fundamental physics
\end{itemize}

The framework partially organizes lepton-sector Yukawa couplings under specific optimization protocols, but quark holdout failure (0\% strict survivors) limits any cross-sector organization claim. The model remains phenomenological throughout. Future work may explore connections to UV physics, but such exploration is beyond the scope of this structural analysis.

\section{Conclusions and Further Study}

We have presented a phenomenological framework for organizing Yukawa couplings through an interference kernel, using three projection \emph{labels} for sector-specific optimization setups. \textbf{Pre-registered strict-survivor diagnostics (100 geometries, diagnostics 21--23) lead with sector failures before partial successes:}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Quark Sector: Model Failure.} \textbf{0\% strict survivors} at PDG-relative tolerances across Gaussian, clockwork, and generalized envelopes (0/5759 on exhaustive legacy + shell-5 geometry re-baseline, diagnostic~30). Charm mass predictions remain order-of-magnitude wrong ($m_c = 19.4 \pm 17.9$ GeV vs.\ 1.27 GeV). Holdout masses and $|V_{ub}|$ do not generalize from training fits.

\item \textbf{Charged Lepton Sector: Partial Success with Holdout Failure.} Legacy full-objective sweeps report $\sim$60\% success at 10\% tolerance, but train/holdout diagnostics (optimize $m_\mu + m_\tau$, hold out $m_e$) find \textbf{1\% strict survivors}---holdout $m_e$ fails structurally even when training masses are perfect.

\item \textbf{Neutrino Sector: Joint Strict 27.8\%.} Joint PMNS + $\Delta m^2$ objectives (diagnostic 28, Tier B2) yield \textbf{27.8\% strict survivors} (22/79 solved geometries)---the headline neutrino result when masses are included. PMNS-only train/holdout strict diagnostics (diagnostic 23) find \textbf{78.9\% PMNS survivors} (100 geometries) as a sub-result that does not survive joint mass constraints. Legacy full-objective archives report $\sim$39--45\% at 10\% tolerance; legacy runs show $\sim$50\% optimization failure ($\theta_{23} \approx 0$), and $g_{\text{env}}$--mixing correlations remain weak.

\item \textbf{Parameter Commonalities: Likely Artifacts.} The apparent consistency in $\sigma \approx 4$--5 and $\alpha \approx \pi$ likely reflects optimization bias toward interior parameter values rather than physical universality.

\item \textbf{Three-Regime Framework: Not Validated.} With the quark sector failing completely, the claim of a ``universal kernel'' sampled differently by each sector is not supported by the data.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Further Study}

Future work is organized by what the pre-registered diagnostic tranche (21--31) has already closed versus what remains testable.

\textbf{Closed within this kernel class (do not repeat without a new ansatz).}
Exhaustive legacy geometry re-optimization (1000 coordinates $0$--$4$ plus 4770 shell-5 geometries, 5759 total) yields \textbf{0\% strict survivors} at PDG-relative tolerances (diagnostic~30). Random extension samples from larger coordinate pools (diagnostic~29), independent $Q_u,Q_d$ relaxations (minimality ladder, diagnostic~09), rank-2 and texture Yukawa ans\"atze (diagnostic~32), and split-fermion overlap derivations of the kernel envelope (diagnostic~33) likewise fail holdout or strict criteria. Envelope-only kernel comparisons (Gaussian, clockwork, generalized $p$; diagnostics~21, 27) do not restore simultaneous quark agreement. These are \emph{structural} negative results, not sampling gaps.

\textbf{Publication package (completed in companion repository).}
Sector-honest manuscript text, repo-locked survivor protocol, frozen diagnostic reports (diagnostics~21--23, 27--28, 30--33, 34--38), and SVD phase-convention repair with unit tests (\texttt{fix\_svd\_phases} preserves $Y=U\Sigma V^\dagger$). Reproduce headline artifacts via \texttt{scripts/reproduce\_phenomenology\_tranche.sh}. Legacy optimizer CSVs are documented in \texttt{data/README.md}.

\textbf{Tier 5 conjecture$\leftrightarrow$physics audits (closed).}
Non-circular explicit-formula holdout (diagnostic~34) confirms zero heights are specific to $\psi(x)$ but is \emph{arithmetic only}, not a flavor mechanism. Jacobi/tridiagonal inverse-kernel tests (diagnostic~35) fail to reduce Yukawas to a 3-site self-adjoint phase model. Optimization-landscape RMT (diagnostic~37) finds Poisson-like Hessian spacings, not GUE universality. Standard QED spectral sums (diagnostic~38) use integer mode indices; prime-only surrogates disagree by 67--93\% with textbook $\zeta$-series targets. No Hilbert--Polya or zeta$\to$CKM claim is supported.

\textbf{CP readout (completed; no large sweeps).}
$\delta_{\text{CKM}}$, $J$, and PMNS CP phases are implemented in the observables pipeline; post phase-fix audit (diagnostic~36, $N{=}15$) finds \textbf{0/15 strict} quark survivors---refutation stable. Full CP optimization grids are deprioritized without a new falsifier.

\textbf{Deprioritized or speculative.}
\textbf{Universal parameters} across sectors remain refuted (transfer and Fisher diagnostics).
\textbf{UV completions and RGE evolution} may motivate effective parameters but do not explain quark holdout failure within the present readout.
\textbf{Information-theoretic sampling} of Yukawa matrices (diagnostics~12--19) showed no operational mechanism for mixing.
Arithmetic--flavor numerology and 3$\times$3 GUE Yukawa tests remain closed (diagnostics~33--35, 38); watch-only routes are indirect (trace formulas, hypothetical HP spectrum).

In summary, the useful forward path is \emph{honest partial phenomenology} (neutrino joint fits, lepton limitations, quark structural failure) and reproducible reporting---not larger discrete geometry catalogs, revived universal-kernel claims, or arithmetic--flavor numerology without a non-circular spectral hook.
